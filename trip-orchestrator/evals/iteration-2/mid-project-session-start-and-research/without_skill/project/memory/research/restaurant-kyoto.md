## Shortlist Summary by Category

### Special Occasion (Michelin)
**Best for:** Memorable dining experience, worth the splurge
- **Kikunoi** — 3-star, vegetable kaiseki available. Booking essential.
- **Hyotei** — 3-star, garden setting, vegetable kaiseki available. Historic (300+ years).

### Traditional Cuisine (Accessible)
**Best for:** Authentic experience, moderate price
- **Omen Kodai-ji** — Yudofu (tofu hot pot), vegetarian-friendly concept, quiet Higashiyama setting
- **Okutan Nishi Hongan-ji** — Historic yudofu, near Hongan-ji temple, good lunch option
- **Misoka-an Kawamichi** — Handmade soba, quiet neighborhood, respects both diets well

### Casual / Backup
**Best for:** Flexible lunch or backup plan
- **Ippudo Ramen** — Reliable chain, vegetable broth option for Jordan
- **Gion Tanto** — Omakase sushi counter, respects seafood tradition

## Key Constraints & Notes

### Vegetarian Challenge
Kyoto's traditional cuisine relies heavily on dashi (fish stock) and bonito. True vegetarian options are less common than Tokyo. However:
- Yudofu (tofu hot pot) establishments explicitly cater to vegetarians — excellent alternative
- Michelin-starred kaiseki often accommodates vegetarian omakase with advance notice
- Ramen houses with vegetable broth exist but are less celebrated

### Booking Strategy
- **Michelin venues (Kikunoi, Hyotei):** Book 2–4 weeks in advance, mention dietary requirement
- **Yudofu & soba:** Walk-in friendly or same-week reservation
- **Sushi counter (Gion Tanto):** Book 1 week ahead, specify Alex's preferences

### Ruled-Out Rationale
- **Ramen Alley:** Geisha district location, mediocre ratings (3.8/5), high tourist density, unreliable
- **Pontocho Kappa Zushi:** Tourist trap (geisha district), mixed reviews despite premium pricing
- **Gourmet Tempura Shimazu:** High-traffic area, variable quality, vegetable tempura less celebrated than protein
