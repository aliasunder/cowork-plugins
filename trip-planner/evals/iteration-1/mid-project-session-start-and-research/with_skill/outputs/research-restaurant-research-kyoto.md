# Restaurant Research — Kyoto

**Date of research:** March 31, 2026
**Trip dates:** Oct 18–21 (4 nights, 5 meals: 4 dinners + 1 lunch)
**Traveler:** Jordan (strict vegetarian — no fish sauce, no dashi with bonito); Alex (omnivore, eats everything)

**Sources consulted:**
- Michelin Guide Kyoto (guide.michelin.com)
- Tabelog (major Japanese restaurant ratings site)
- TripAdvisor, Google Maps, Inside Kyoto, Veggies Abroad, Never Ending Voyage
- Local food blogs (Japanese Taste, Japan Wonder Travel, byFood)

## Scope & Criteria

**Hard filters:**
- Must accommodate strict vegetarian for Jordan (no fish-based broths, no fish sauce)
- Dinner options within walking distance from hotel (or accessible by Uber/taxi)
- Booking required (Kyoto fills up, especially during Oct — advance reservations essential)
- Recent reviews (2025–2026) confirming service quality

**Nice-to-haves:**
- Traditional cuisine (Kyoto is known for refined regional cooking)
- Shojin ryori (Buddhist vegetarian cuisine) for Jordan — a signature Kyoto experience
- Range of price points (mix of fine dining and mid-range)
- Character/ambiance — Alex appreciates authentic, not touristy chains

**Budget:** $50–150 USD per person per meal; willing to splurge on 1 special dinner

## Key Insights

1. **Shojin ryori is Jordan's ideal dining experience in Kyoto.** It's Buddhist temple vegetarian cuisine, fully vegan, and culturally significant. Options include Shigetsu (2-star Michelin, Arashiyama), Daitokuji Ikkyu (500+ years old, central), Ajiro Honten (longest-hold Michelin vegan star), and Izusen Daiji-in (multi-course temple lunch).

2. **Most traditional kaiseki restaurants are fish-heavy and problematic for Jordan.** While Michelin-starred options like Hyotei and Kitcho are world-class, they center on dashi, bonito, and fish. Attempting vegetarian substitutions would compromise the intended experience. Shojin ryori is the vegetarian-first equivalent in Kyoto.

3. **October is peak season.** Advance reservations (2–4 weeks) are essential, especially for Michelin-starred and temple restaurants.

4. **Alex has omnivore-friendly options.** While dining around Jordan's restrictions, Alex can enjoy refined Kyoto cuisine at shojin ryori restaurants (many include meat-based sides or options for non-vegetarians).

5. **Day-by-day logistics:**
   - Oct 18 (arrival): Skip or casual if energy permits
   - Oct 19: Temple shojin ryori dinner (Ikkyu or Ajiro)
   - Oct 19–20: Daytime shojin ryori lunch option (Izusen, temple setting)
   - Oct 20: Arashiyama-area shojin ryori (Shigetsu) if not done Day 1
   - Oct 21: Repeat temple dining or casual ramen

6. **Booking urgency:** Email Shigetsu, Daitokuji Ikkyu, and Ajiro Honten immediately to secure spots. These fill 2–4 weeks out, especially for premium times (7–8pm seatings).
