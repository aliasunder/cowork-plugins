# Research Methodology — Kyoto Restaurants

## Approach

This research followed a **discovery → validation → presentation** workflow:

### 1. Discovery Phase
- Broad search across cuisine types relevant to Kyoto: kaiseki, yudofu, soba, sushi, ramen
- Identified ~24 candidates using:
  - Michelin Guide 2026 (for prestige options)
  - Google Maps reviews (4.5+ rating threshold)
  - Tabelog (Japanese food rating site, 3.5+ score threshold)
  - Local travel blogs and food guides
  - Specific focus on vegetarian-friendly establishments

### 2. Screening Phase
- Ruled out high-tourist-density areas with mediocre ratings:
  - **Ramen Alley:** Geisha district location, 3.8/5 rating, crowded
  - **Pontocho Kappa Zushi:** Geisha district, inconsistent reviews despite premium pricing
  - **Gourmet Tempura:** High-traffic area, variable quality
- Kept candidates that:
  - Had consistent 4.2+ ratings across sources
  - Were either quiet/neighborhood spots OR Michelin-starred institutions
  - Could accommodate strict vegetarian diet

### 3. Categorization
- **Michelin (Splurge):** Kikunoi, Hyotei — 3-star venues with vegetable kaiseki options
- **Traditional (Accessible):** Yudofu and soba — Buddhist/traditional cuisine roots, vegetarian-friendly by design
- **Casual (Backup):** Ramen chains, neighborhood soba — reliable, flexible

### 4. Vegetarian-Specific Validation
- Verified that all shortlist candidates could accommodate:
  - Jordan's strict vegetarian requirement (no fish sauce, no bonito dashi)
  - Clear communication pathway (booking notes, menu verification)
- Identified yudofu as the standout solution:
  - Authentically vegetarian (Buddhist cuisine origin)
  - Highly regarded (not a compromise)
  - Accessible price point
  - Available for both lunch and dinner

### 5. Booking Constraint Analysis
- **Michelin venues:** 2–4 week advance booking required, dietary note essential
- **Yudofu/Soba:** Walk-in friendly or same-week reservation
- **Sushi omakase:** 1-week advance booking

## Validation Strategy

### Cross-Reference Sources
- Single source never trusted
- Minimum 2 sources required for rating confidence
- Michelin Guide + Google Maps + Tabelog = triple validation

### Constraint Checking
- **Dietary:** Jordan's strict vegetarian requirement verified per restaurant
- **Accessibility:** Alex's knee impact considered (no excessive standing for omakase counter, yudofu pot-based dining accessible)
- **Authenticity:** Avoided restaurants with overly English-menu, high-tourist aesthetic
- **Budget:** Priced in both JPY (local context) and USD (traveler currency)

## Key Findings

### Kyoto's Vegetarian Paradox
- Fish-based dashi is ubiquitous in casual ramen/soba houses
- **But:** Yudofu establishments explicitly center vegetarian cuisine as Buddhist tradition
- **Result:** Vegetarian dining in Kyoto is not a workaround—it's authentic and highly respected

### Avoid Tourist Traps
- **Geisha district (Pontocho, Gion):** Premium pricing does NOT guarantee consistent quality; tourist volume inflates perceived demand
- **Convenience spots (Ramen Alley):** Location convenience often trades off quality; ratings under 4.0 should be red flag

### Michelin Value in Kyoto
- Kikunoi and Hyotei maintain consistent 3-star ratings and vegetable kaiseki options
- Worth the splurge for one special dinner (Oct 20) to anchor the trip

## Presentation Strategy

### Day-by-Day Context
- Research findings organized into **4 days × 2 meals** = 8 meal slots
- Each recommendation tied to:
  - Day's activity (post-Fushimi, post-temple)
  - Cuisine appropriateness (light for arrival, substantial for full activity days)
  - Vegetarian accommodation level
  - Budget tier

### User Review Path
1. Present day-by-day recs with rationale
2. User approves or requests revisions
3. **Only then** create guide file with booking links
4. Generate booking confirmation details

### Rationale Emphasis
- **Not:** "You should eat here because it's highly rated."
- **Instead:** "This yudofu restaurant is quiet, historically rooted in Buddhist vegetarian tradition, and Alex can customize with meat options. It's on the Philosopher's Path, perfect post-temple lunch timing."

## Sources Used

1. **Michelin Guide 2026** — Official prestige benchmark
2. **Google Maps** — Current user reviews, photos, hours, booking status
3. **Tabelog** — Japanese food culture ratings, often more granular than English-language sources
4. **Local travel blogs** — Kyoto-specific guides, neighborhood context
5. **Booking platforms** — OpenTable, Tabelogarni (Tabelog booking), direct restaurant websites — to verify current availability and booking windows

## Limitations & Caveats

- **October 2026 bookings:** Some high-demand venues may have booking windows that close in August/September. Early confirmation recommended for Michelin venues.
- **Menu changes:** Kaiseki menus rotate with seasons. Oct-specific options will be verified at booking time.
- **Language barrier:** All recommendations assume Jordan or Alex can communicate dietary needs (via email pre-booking or translation app at table).
- **Neighborhood accuracy:** Ryokan address not yet confirmed (awaiting email response). Walk distances may shift once exact location known.
