# Destination Guide — Japan in October

**Overview:** Japan in October is shoulder season — mild weather, fewer crowds than peak seasons (spring/summer or November foliage), good conditions for temple and garden exploration. This guide covers practical information for your trip.

---

## Weather & What to Pack

### October Climate

| City | Avg Temp | Humidity | Precipitation | Notes |
|------|----------|----------|----------------|-------|
| **Kyoto** | 55–68°F (13–20°C) | Moderate | Low, occasional rain | Mild, pleasant. Layers essential. |
| **Osaka** | 55–68°F (13–20°C) | Moderate | Low, occasional rain | Similar to Kyoto. Slightly warmer. |
| **Tokyo** | 50–66°F (10–19°C) | Moderate | Low, occasional rain | Slightly cooler than Kyoto. Variable wind. |

### Packing Essentials
- **Layers:** Light sweater, fleece, or light jacket. October mornings cool, afternoons mild.
- **Rain jacket or compact umbrella:** Occasional rain, not constant.
- **Comfortable walking shoes:** Break in before trip. You'll walk ~15k–20k steps/day.
- **Socks:** Many temples/ryokans require shoe removal. Bring extra clean socks.
- **Modest clothing:** Temples require covered shoulders, long pants preferred (but knees OK). Avoid very short shorts, low-cut tops, sleeveless in religious spaces.
- **Hat/sunglasses:** Sun can be strong, especially at temples with limited shade.

### Alex's Knee-Specific Notes
- **Comfortable walking shoes essential** — break in thoroughly. Knee prefers stability over cushioning.
- **Consider: Support socks or compression sleeve** — optional, but helps with long-day swelling.
- **Accessible shoes for tatami:** Slip-ons are faster to remove/put on. Laces take time when entering temples frequently.

## Practical Information

### Money

**Currency:** Japanese Yen (¥). ~100 ¥ = $0.70 USD (October 2026 rate TBD, but approximate baseline).

**Cash vs. Card:**
- Japan is **cash-first** outside major cities. Most temples, smaller restaurants, convenience stores cash-only.
- Carry **¥10,000–20,000 (~$70–150)** at any time. Top up at convenience store ATMs (7-Eleven, FamilyMart, Lawson).
- **Credit/debit cards** work at major hotels, department stores, some restaurants in cities.
- **No tipping** — ever. Tip is considered insulting. Bill includes tax.

**Budget per day (approximation):**
- Meals: $25–35 (mix cheap ramen + nicer dinner).
- Temple/garden entries: $5–10.
- Transport: $5–10.
- Misc: $5–15.
- **Total: ~$50–70/day on average.**

### Phones & Internet

**Staying Connected:**
- **SIM card:** Buy prepaid SIM at airport (Haneda/Narita) or convenience store. Data ~¥500–1000 ($3–7) for 7 days.
- **WiFi:** Hotels, convenience stores, Starbucks offer free WiFi. Coverage varies.
- **Roaming:** Check with your US carrier; usually expensive. Bring offline translation app.

**Useful Apps (download before trip or use offline):**
- Google Translate (photo translation, offline mode).
- Google Maps (offline maps available).
- Hyperdia or Yahoo Transit (train schedules, English-friendly).
- Suica/Pasmo reader (if you get transit card).

### Tipping, Politeness & Customs

**Tipping:** **Do NOT tip.** Tipping is not expected and can be offensive in Japan. All bills include tax and service.

**Politeness:**
- **Bow slightly** when greeting or thanking (not mandatory, appreciated).
- **Remove shoes** at temples, ryokans, some traditional homes.
- **Speak quietly** on trains/in public. Phone conversations are taboo on transit.
- **Don't point sticks** (chopsticks) at people or stand them upright in rice (funeral custom).
- **Photos:** Ask before photographing people. Temples sometimes prohibit photos in sacred areas; look for signs.

**Etiquette at Temples:**
- Remove shoes on tatami mats.
- Bow before passing through torii gate (Shinto shrine).
- Speak quietly, don't run.
- Some inner sanctums prohibit photography — check for signs.

### Accessibility Notes (Alex)

**Stairs & Elevators:**
- **Kyoto:** Historic, narrow streets with occasional stairs. Elevators in modern buildings, hotels. Temples can have steps; research beforehand.
- **Tokyo:** Mostly flat, excellent elevator coverage in stations/malls. Stairs in older neighborhoods.
- **Osaka:** Mixed; central areas walkable, some stairs in older districts.

**Metro Systems:**
- **Kyoto:** Modest metro system, some stairs. Taxis often easier.
- **Tokyo:** Extensive metro, good elevator coverage. English signage.
- **Osaka:** Good coverage, similar to Tokyo.

**Practical Tips for Alex:**
- **Use taxis after long activity days** rather than forcing metro/buses when knee is fatigued.
- **Rest at 1–2pm** — mid-day break in hotel or café prevents end-of-day pain.
- **Elevators at hotels** — confirm when booking. Request low floor if available.
- **Garden seating areas** — most Japanese gardens have benches; build in rest time.

### Transportation Overview

**Trains & Railways:**
- **JR (Japan Railways):** National railway. Reliable, efficient, extensive.
- **JR Pass:** Unlimited travel option for 7, 14, or 21 consecutive days. Cost: ~¥30,000–50,000 (≈ $210–350). Worth it if making 3+ intercity trips.
- **Suica/Pasmo:** Reloadable transit cards for local trains, buses. Buy at station, reload at convenience stores.

**Within Cities:**
- **Trains/Metro:** Fastest, most frequent. Signage often English.
- **Buses:** Good coverage but stairs required, less English. Use if convenient.
- **Taxis:** Expensive (~¥2,000–4,000 / $15–30 per ride in city) but comfortable. Good option for tired evenings.
- **Walking:** Flat central areas are very walkable.

**Key Routes for Your Trip (examples):**
- **Airports to cities:**
  - Haneda (Tokyo): Monorail or Keikyu Line (~30 min to central Tokyo).
  - Narita (Tokyo): N'EX train (~60 min) or Skyliner (~60 min, faster).
  - Kansai (Osaka): Haruka Express (~75 min to Kyoto), fastest option.

- **Intercity:**
  - Kyoto ↔ Osaka: Local train, ~75 min.
  - Kyoto ↔ Tokyo: Tokaido Shinkansen (bullet train), ~2 hrs, expensive (~¥13,000/$90) but fast & comfortable.

## Temples vs. Shrines (Quick Reference)

| Aspect | **Shinto Shrine** | **Buddhist Temple** |
|--------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Religion** | Shintoism (indigenous Japanese) | Buddhism |
| **Focus** | Nature spirits, ancestors, divinity | Meditation, enlightenment, statues/art |
| **Symbol** | Orange/red torii gate | Bell, lotus, pagoda |
| **Entry** | Often free (small donation OK) | Usually ¥300–800 ($2–6) entry fee |
| **Examples** | Meiji Shrine (Tokyo), Fushimi Inari (Kyoto) | Kinkaku-ji (Kyoto), Senso-ji (Tokyo) |
| **Etiquette** | Bow at torii, clap before praying | Remove shoes on tatami, quiet contemplation |

**For your trip:** Both are valuable. Temples are your priority (Buddhism + architecture + gardens), but shrines offer different cultural nuance.

## Dining Basics (Quick Guide)

### How to Order

**Pointing:** Photos on menus are common; point to what you want.
**English:** Many restaurants have English menus or staff in cities. Smaller places might not.
**Phrasebook essentials:**
- "Vegetarian dishes, please" → *Shokutsuhin o kudasai* (vegetarian focus).
- "No fish/meat, please" → *Sakana nashi, niku nashi* (no fish, no meat).
- "Allergies?" → *Allerugī aru ka?*
- "Check/bill, please" → *Okaikei onegaishimasu*.

### Payment

**Cash preferred.** Many small restaurants don't take cards. ATMs at convenience stores take US cards.
**No tipping.** Ever.
**Bathroom:** Often free and clean. Look for WC sign or toilet symbol.

### Vegetarian Dining

**Good options:**
- **Shojin ryori** (Buddhist vegetarian cuisine) — elegant, plant-based, often served at temples.
- **Ramen** with vegetable broth (not tonkotsu/pork or chicken).
- **Edamame, cucumber, pickled vegetables.**
- **Sushi** with vegetarian rolls (cucumber, avocado, pickled radish).
- **Noodles (udon, soba)** often have vegetable broth option.
- **Convenience stores** have lots of vegetarian options (salads, onigiri, snacks).

**Places to avoid if strict vegetarian:** Izakaya (often meat/fish focused), traditional sushi (fish), tonkotsu ramen (pork broth).

## October Calendar & Local Events

- **Early October:** Preparation for autumn festivals (varies by region).
- **Mid-October:** Some regional festivals (Takayama Matsuri, Aoi Matsuri afterglow).
- **Late October:** Winding down before November foliage peak (crowds less intense).

**School/Work:** October is regular season; no major holidays in Japan (unlike Golden Week in May or New Year).

## Quick Dos & Don'ts

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Learn basic phrases (respect to locals). | Expect English everywhere (learn backup strategies). |
| Use chopsticks to eat (not ideal, but try). | Use chopsticks to play/point. |
| Bow when greeting or thanking. | Shake hands (uncommon; bow preferred). |
| Remove shoes on tatami mats. | Wear shoes on tatami. |
| Be quiet on trains/in public. | Talk loudly, take phone calls on transit. |
| Carry cash for small purchases. | Rely entirely on cards. |
| Research temples/gardens beforehand. | Show up expecting everything to be open (hours vary). |
| Ask permission before photographing. | Photograph people without asking. |
| Respect rope/sign barriers at temples. | Cross barriers or ignore "no entry" signs. |
| Enjoy the journey at a slow pace. | Rush between sites like a checklist. |
